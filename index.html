Case study — Music & Creator Growth

From Listener
to Artist

How AI music platforms can turn passive listeners into creators, and creators into artists — by removing friction at the right time.

Xinru Li
2026
Background

On music and access

From founding Chicago's Teen Arts Council to conducting orchestras in public parks, lowering the barrier to the arts has been a constant for over a decade. The clearest example: starting the Meta Orchestra at Meta's New York office.

Because we rehearse where people already work, we've recruited accidental musicians — people who wandered in and ended up staying past 9PM to practice an instrument they'd just picked up. In a company that prizes efficiency above almost everything, it struck me how readily people chose to commit to something hard once the access was there.

Suno is the first product I've seen that could replicate that effect at scale.

"It struck me how readily people chose to deeply commit to something hard — once the initial access and community was there."

— On founding the Meta Orchestra, NYC
The proposal

A three-stage pipeline to make anyone a musician

No one falls in love with the violin on day one. The path in is always low friction first: a taste, a moment of community, then the deeper commitment that brings fulfillment.

Lower the barrier to try. Give people a reason to come back. Then let them go as deep as they want.

The tension

Art still requires taste and a point of view — no matter how much AI is involved. The goal isn't to remove that depth. It's to make sure people discover it.

01 — What's broken

The creation flow asks too much, too soon

Suno asks users to describe in text exactly what they want — genre, mood, feel. For someone with a clear musical vision, that's powerful. For everyone else, it's a wall. The users most likely to become loyal creators are the ones least equipped to get past it.

The random generator helps — it removes the blank page. But it produces something generic, disconnected from who the user is or what they love. Personal connection is what turns a one-time try into a habit. That's what the two solutions below are designed to create.

02 — Lowering the barrier

Give people something to react to, not invent from scratch

Creation is easier when you have a starting point. Both solutions give users something personal to react to, rather than something to invent from scratch.

Solution A — Contextual feed prompt

Start from something you already love

A prompt surfaces in the feed, built from listening history: "You've been replaying this vibe — want to make one?" Parameters are pre-filled. The leap shrinks from "what do I make?" to "do I want to try?" — a question most people say yes to.

Solution B — Video-to-song

Start from something you already have

Upload a video — a birthday, a trip, a homemade pie — and Suno generates a song that fits it. Billions of people upload videos every day. Almost none have written a music prompt. Meeting users on familiar ground removes the intimidation and produces something that feels personal from the first note.

Creator analytics — listens, accounts reached, avg. listen time, and follows

03 — Creator analytics

Creating once is an experiment. Twice is a habit — if you hear something back.

Creating once is an experiment. Twice is a habit — but only if something happens in between. Suno shows hearts and comments, but reacting requires effort a passive viewer rarely gives. For early creators, silence feels like disappearing. That's what stops the second song.

The fix is signal, not social mechanics. An MVP dashboard showing listens, accounts reached, average listen time, and follows gives a creator something to react to — proof that real people heard their work, even if nobody left a heart. That's often enough to make the next song.

04 — North star

Scroller to creator is solved.
Now make them an artist.

Everything above moves someone from passive listener to active creator. That's the first half. The north star is the second.

A creator makes things. An artist has a practice and an audience. Getting there requires two things Suno doesn't yet offer: tools to go deeper than prompting, and a reason to treat it as something worth investing in.

A standalone creation app

TikTok has CapCut. Instagram has Edits. Once a consumption platform reaches scale, a dedicated creation tool unlocks the next tier of depth. Suno Studio exists on desktop — but committed creators aren't at laptops. A mobile-first app with multi-track arrangement, stem editing, and one-tap publishing gives Suno a creator-first identity alongside the consumption product.

Creator monetization

Fulfillment brings people. Sustainability keeps them. Once a creator has an audience, Suno can introduce revenue sharing tied to real signals: total plays, attribution when your song inspires another, reach on video content. Art that started as self-expression becomes something worth investing in. That's when a creator becomes an artist.
